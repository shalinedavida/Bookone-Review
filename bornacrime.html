    Love & Relationships

    Romantic love: Trevor's first ever romantic crush(love) was a girl named Zaheera, whom
    he found very beautiful, and as he described her, a silent, shy, and well-mannered girl,
    whom he made a friend afraid of rejection, and with the thought of her being out of his
    league. And he was thinking about working on it in the long run, thinking that he would
    have more time to spend with her, which eventually wasn’t the case, and she left the
    country to go abroad. And later on, he learns from her friends that she was interested in
    him, which taught him a lesson about acting on time and to have courage.

    A lesson that Trevor highlights in the book that he learnt was that you do not own the
    people you love . This lesson was brought about by his love for his dog Fufi.He
    mentions how Fufi could do a bunch of things, like jumping over the perimeter wall
    during the day and going out to some other kid's house. The day that he followed Fufi
    and found this out, it hurt him so much that he felt like he was being cheated on . In his
    head, he loved Fufi, which meant Fufi was his, but in a real sense, Fufi was just a dog
    that he loved, and Fufi was allowed a life of his own. This story comes up as an
    important lesson he learnt in life, which he later applied to his personal romantic life. He
    learnt that you just have to love people, and the people you love have no obligation
    toward you and can have a life of their own because they do not belong to you just
    because you love them.

    Familial love: growing up with his mom, Trevor learnt the love that his mother had for
    him and the sacrifices that she made to raise him well and to show him what he
    deserves. There was this quote from the book which he used to describe that, which
    was “Love is a creative act. When you love someone you create a new world for them.
    My mother did that for me, and with the progress I made and the things I learned, I
    came back and created a new world and a new understanding for her.”

    Feeling Like an Outsider

    Trevor Noah, both as an individual and a celebrity, recounts a part of his school life
    where he felt invisible. In his book, he reflects on his lack of popularity and the
    absence of a defined clique, contrasting his experience with his peers, such as the
    white students who gathered together or the black kids from the township who
    played soccer together. As the only mixed-race student among a school population
    of over a thousand, he often felt alone and out of place. As Trevor Noah states in his
    book, “It is easier to be an insider as an outsider than to be an outsider as an insider”

    This experience resonates strongly in today's world and generation, particularly for
    international students. For instance, being a Black African student in a study-abroad
    program poses unique challenges that many may not fully understand. The
    distinctions between you and other students become painfully apparent, and
    sometimes, even fellow Black students may create a divide, perceiving a difference
    between themselves as African Americans and you as an African student.

    To cope with these challenges, many African students tend to form supportive
    groups among themselves. Additionally, seeking therapy sessions has become a
    common strategy, helping them navigate the feelings of isolation often experienced
    in a foreign environment.

    Another instance of feeling like an anomaly individually is being raised by
    guardians . This came up from a discussion where an experience of being raised
    in an extended family took away the opportunity for familiarity among the
    household members. This led to the feeling of being the odd one out, where you
    tend to feel like you are not recognized and you are not seen . In the context of
    Trevoh Noah’s book, I highly resonate with his experiences where he felt like he
    did not fit in and created his little world for himself. This was how he coped with
    the fact that he was among people, yet alone ,this tends to happen frequently.

    Patricia as a Protagonist

    Patricia acts as the sole caregiver and protector to Trevor. Raising Trevor she is a strict
    mother, but she is strict with him in order to impact positive values in Trevor. She
    believes being strict with Trevor is what helps in shaping him into a better person
    with values. These values that Trevor learns guide who he becomes a person and
    instills discipline in him. Particia is also hard working and does what she can to
    ensure that they are able to support themselves financially. This character inspires
    Trevor and pushes him in his career. Trevor also comes out as hardworking and
    does everything needed to achieve his goal. For example, in his CD and DJ job,
    where he gets local fame, Trevor runs the business with hard work and passion. It
    is through his actions that he is able to see his business develop and grow.
    Patricia’s resilience also teaches Trevor to always stand on what is right. She goes
    against various societal expectations and does what she thinks is right.

    When Trevor makes a mistake in school instead of reprimanding or punishing him,
    Patricia weighs on the matter and uses it to impact meaningful lessons to Trevor.
    There is a time that Trevor takes the communion which he is supposed not to take.
    He gets reprimanded by the school. When Patricia gets to know of this, she
    addresses the matter by thinking it is not right to deny a child that. Patricia did not
    just follow the rules but instead questioned them and only abided by what was
    right. Through all these, Trevor learns to always stand up for what is right.

    Additionally, raising Trevor as a mixed race child during the apartheid times is very
    challenging to Patricia. This is most especially due to the fact that she is the sole
    protector to Trevor and mixed people in the society were viewed differently.Despite
    the struggles, she goes through all means to ensure that Trevor is raised well. She
    does what she can to ensure that Trevor gets only what is best. It is through
    Patricia that Trevor learns English as she gets him books to read and also teaches
    him. This is also seen when she takes Trevor to a private school that is majorly for
    the whites so that he can get better education. She also takes different kinds of
    jobs to make ends meet and provide for the needs of Trevor. All these measures
    ensure that Trevor gets a good upbringing. It also molds him into being able to
    interact well with diverse people. She allows Trevor to explore things she never did
    and most blacks were not allowed to.

    Indentity: Given vs Chosen

    Trevor’s existence in the first place is a crime since it was not allowed for people to marry from
    different races.Under apartheid regime Trevor is classified as coloured a category that set him
    apart from his black Mother Patricia and his white father Robert. This identity dictates where
    Trevor can live with his associates and how the people view him.For
    instance when Patricia take Trevor to stay with his grandmother so that he can be safe,while he
    is the grandmothers place is detained to play with the childrens around him because if the police
    could spot it could be a case hence this affected Trevor which made him to feel discriminated
    and also he could not walk with his mother openly.

    Despite the limitations of the assigned identity,Trevor put more effort into defining himself on his
    own terms. His mother took a huge role to teach him how to fight the societal limitation to define
    his own path of being independent and emphasis on faith. He used his adaptability and honour
    to navigate different social groups since Trevor could even talk different languages that is
    English,Xhosa and Africaners; this was a strength that helped him to manoeuvre among the
    groups interactively while breaking the boundaries of race and culture.
    Trevor’s journey is one of self-improvement as he continues to learn to restore the identity
    assigned to him and the identity he chooses to become and he also comprehends the strength
    and determination to maintain and express their unique identity even when faced with social and
    cultural pressure to adapt.
